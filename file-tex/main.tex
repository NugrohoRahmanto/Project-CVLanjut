\begin{abstract}
Autonomous-driving perception systems commonly rely on closed-set detectors whose outputs are limited to categories observed during supervised training. This constraint can produce safety-critical blind spots when road scenes contain vulnerable users, traffic-control objects, or rare hazards outside the learned label space. This paper evaluates open-vocabulary hazard perception by comparing YOLOv8, YOLOv8-World, and a YOLOv8-World retention strategy on a curated BDD10K road-scene subset. The dataset is partitioned into three Target Classes, consisting of car, bus, and truck, and seven Unknown Classes, consisting of pedestrian, rider, train, motorcycle, bicycle, traffic light, and traffic sign. Models are evaluated across Small, Medium, and Large scales under pretrained, all-class, target-class, and retention settings. The evaluation protocol separates all-class accuracy, known-class adaptation, and unknown-class hazard recovery, allowing closed-set failure and open-vocabulary transfer to be measured under the same validation split. The retention strategy combines manually retained Target-Class labels with pseudo-labels generated by a pretrained YOLOv8-World model for Unknown Classes. In the fully supervised 10-class setting, YOLOv8-World-L achieves 33.50\% $mAP_{50}$, compared with 31.77\% for YOLOv8-L. However, ordinary target-only fine-tuning suppresses unknown-class detection, whereas YOLOv8-World retention training recovers 14.04\% unknown-class $mAP_{50}$ at the Large scale, close to the 14.79\% zero-shot reference. These results show that prompt-conditioned retention can preserve broader road-object awareness while reducing silent closed-set failures in autonomous-driving perception.
\end{abstract}

The rapid advancement of autonomous driving systems relies heavily on the accuracy and robustness of perception modules, particularly object detection \cite{Jebamikyous2022Autonomous, Pravallika2024Deep}. Historically, industry standards have favored closed-set object detection models, such as the widely adopted You Only Look Once architectures \cite{Redmon2015You, Terven2023A}. These models are trained on large, meticulously annotated datasets to achieve real-time inference and high precision for predefined categories. However, their operational capacity is strictly bounded by the classes explicitly registered during the training phase.

Real-world driving environments are dynamic and continuously expose perception systems to edge cases outside standard training distributions. Large-scale benchmarks such as Cityscapes \cite{Cordts2016Cityscapes}, the India Driving Dataset \cite{Varma2018IDD}, ACDC \cite{Sakaridis2021ACDC}, and BDD100K \cite{Yu2018BDD100K} capture diverse urban layouts, traffic structures, weather conditions, and times of day. However, even with this dataset coverage, traditional closed-set models still fail to detect out-of-distribution objects or force them into known categories when those objects are absent from training \cite{Dhamija2020The, Li2025Out-of-distribution}. In autonomous navigation, this limitation creates safety hazards because planning systems may remain blind to critical obstacles and corner cases \cite{Li2022CODA, Chen2025INSIGHT}.

To mitigate the vulnerabilities of closed-set paradigms, the field is shifting toward Vision-Language Models and Open-Vocabulary Object Detection frameworks \cite{Radford2021Learning, Li2024From}. By aligning visual features with natural-language embeddings, these frameworks can recognize categories specified through text prompts without domain-specific retraining. YOLO-World \cite{Cheng2024YOLO-World}, implemented in this study through Ultralytics YOLOv8-World checkpoints, combines YOLO efficiency with open-vocabulary, zero-shot detection.




The primary goal of this research is to evaluate whether open-vocabulary object detection can mitigate safety hazards caused by closed-set limitations in autonomous driving. Specifically, YOLOv8-World is compared with YOLOv8 when confronted with unfamiliar or deliberately unlearned objects on a BDD100K-derived subset referred to as BDD10K.

Throughout this paper, the term \textit{Target Classes} denotes object categories that are explicitly registered, learned, and optimized during the model training or fine-tuning phase. Conversely, \textit{Unknown Classes} refer to out-of-distribution road objects that are deliberately excluded from supervised optimization and encountered strictly during inference. Although these concepts are broadly applicable to open-vocabulary detection, this study defines the Target Classes exclusively as three vehicle categories, namely car, bus, and truck. The Unknown Classes comprise the remaining seven unlearned road-hazard categories: pedestrian, rider, train, motorcycle, bicycle, traffic light, and traffic sign.

To achieve this goal, the study follows the pipeline in Fig. \ref{fig:fig_1}. The BDD10K subset is pre-processed through category alias mapping and label normalization, organized into all-class and target-class settings, and evaluated across Small, Medium, and Large variants. YOLOv8-World additionally receives semantic class prompts, while the retention setting combines target labels with pseudo-labels for Unknown Classes. Evaluation combines $mAP_{50}$, $mAP_{50-95}$, known-class, unknown-class, and qualitative detection analysis.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

The evolution of real-time object detection has been significantly shaped by the You Only Look Once architectures \cite{Redmon2015You, Terven2023A, Diwan2022Object, Vijayakumar2024YOLO-based}, which traditionally operate under closed-vocabulary assumptions and require predefined categories. Recent open-vocabulary and zero-shot detectors, especially YOLO-World \cite{Cheng2024YOLO-World}, extend this paradigm by combining vision-language models with prompt-based localization \cite{Radford2021Learning, Gu2021Open-vocabulary, Du2022Learning}. This shift enables detection of categories specified through language prompts and responds to dynamic environments where unknown classes frequently appear \cite{Iqbal2024Redefining, Li2024From, Schmarje2024UNCOVER}.

Robust autonomous-driving detection also depends on diverse evaluation data, including BDD100K \cite{Yu2018BDD100K}, Cityscapes \cite{Cordts2016Cityscapes}, IDD \cite{Varma2018IDD}, ACDC \cite{Sakaridis2021ACDC}, and CODA \cite{Li2022CODA}, which expose models to urban variation, adverse conditions, and road corner cases \cite{Liu2024Benchmarking, Chhipa2024Open-Vocabulary}. Efficiency-oriented driving studies report useful closed-set references: Gupta et al. \cite{10605392} obtain BDD10K mAP values of 20.08--23.35\%, Geever et al. \cite{11376542} show that downsampling choices change average precision by less than 2\%, and Li et al. \cite{10550965} improve vehicle detection under difficult lighting and spatial constraints. The BDD100K model zoo further reports 10-class Box AP values of 32.71\%, 33.72\%, and 31.13\% for Faster R-CNN R-101-FPN, Cascade R-CNN R-50-FPN, and FCOS R-101-FPN, respectively \cite{bdd100kModelZoo}; however, these references still primarily reflect known-class detection.

Zero-shot and prompt-guided models have also been adapted beyond autonomous driving. Mullins et al. \cite{agronomy14122830} evaluated YOLO-World and Grounding DINO for wild blueberry annotation, where Grounding DINO achieved 0.642 mIoU compared with 0.503 mIoU for YOLO-World. In manufacturing, Tang et al. \cite{Tang2025Prompt} applied prompt-guided AI analytics to digital twins, showing that vision-language detection can transfer to specialized industrial objects. These studies demonstrate broad flexibility, but their domain shift, computational overhead, and imperfect zero-shot precision leave open questions for safety-critical road scenes.

Despite these advances, open-vocabulary detection has not been sufficiently validated against established closed-set standards for Unknown Class autonomous-driving hazards. Existing literature largely reports known-class detection accuracy, domain-transfer results, or efficiency trade-offs, while the safety-critical question of whether a detector can recover road objects excluded from training remains unresolved. Therefore, this research directly compares YOLOv8-World with YOLOv8 on BDD10K under controlled open-vocabulary and closed-set evaluation protocols.


\section{Research Methodology}
\subsection{Research Architecture}


The comparative models differ primarily in input modality and classification paradigm, as conceptualized in Fig. \ref{fig:fig_2}. YOLOv8, shown in Fig. \ref{fig:fig_2}b, follows a closed-set single-modality design that maps visual features to fixed classification heads learned during training; therefore, out-of-distribution objects are either ignored as background or forced into known labels. YOLOv8-World, shown in Fig. \ref{fig:fig_2}a, uses an open-vocabulary dual-modality design that conditions visual detection on semantic text prompts, enabling novel object localization without structural retraining.

To evaluate this disparity in autonomous driving, the study uses the BDD10K multi-scheme pipeline in Fig. \ref{fig:fig_1} across Small (s), Medium (m), and Large (l) variants. The all-class track trains both architectures on the full 10-class set to establish a supervised upper bound, while the Target-Class track trains only on three vehicle classes and withholds seven Unknown Classes. In the latter setting, YOLOv8-World is queried for Unknown Classes through text prompts, whereas YOLOv8 is analyzed through class-agnostic recall, forced misclassification, and miss-rate behavior.

\subsection{Dataset Configuration}
This study uses BDD10K, a curated and label-normalized subset derived from BDD100K. Model optimization uses the standard \texttt{train} split, while validation, zero-shot testing, and metric computation use only the \texttt{val} split because the official BDD100K \texttt{test} labels are not publicly available for exact bounding-box evaluation. The ten mapped categories are divided into three Target Classes, namely car, bus, and truck, and seven Unknown Classes, namely pedestrian, rider, train, motorcycle, bicycle, traffic light, and traffic sign. As summarized in Table~\ref{tab:bdd10k_class_distribution}, Target Classes represent approximately 64.5\% of training instances and 64.0\% of combined train-validation instances, while Unknown Classes account for 35.5\% and 36.0\%. This 3:7 split is specific to this study and emulates a safety-critical setting in which dominant vehicle classes are supervised while vulnerable road users, traffic-control devices, and rare hazards must remain detectable.

In the Ultralytics implementation, the YOLOv8-World variants correspond to \texttt{YOLOv8s-world}, \texttt{YOLOv8m-world}, and \texttt{YOLOv8l-world}. \subsection{Training Methodology}
Six experimental schemes are evaluated across Small, Medium, and Large variants: frozen pretrained YOLOv8-World, YOLOv8 10-Class, YOLOv8 3-Class, YOLOv8-World 10-Class, YOLOv8-World 3-Class, and YOLOv8-World 3-Class Retention. In the ordinary Target-Class setting, only car, bus, and truck remain as positive labels, while the seven Unknown Class annotations are removed and absorbed into background loss. The retention scheme in Fig.~\ref{fig:fig_retention_flow} reduces this loss of open-vocabulary awareness by combining retained Target-Class labels with pretrained YOLOv8-World pseudo-labels for Unknown Classes; therefore, it provides weak supervision for unlearned hazards without using full manual 10-class annotation.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}
Evaluation is performed on the BDD10K \texttt{val} split in three complementary stages. The all-class stage measures performance across the complete 10-class prompt set or closed-set head. The known-class stage isolates car, bus, and truck to quantify how strongly each model adapts to the Target Classes. The unknown-class stage isolates pedestrian, rider, train, motorcycle, bicycle, traffic light, and traffic sign to measure hazard recovery beyond the supervised target vocabulary. For the 3-class YOLOv8 model, all Unknown Class outputs are mathematically unavailable, so detections for those categories are counted as zero and the corresponding ground-truth objects become false negatives.

The evaluation therefore separates supervised adaptation from open-vocabulary retention. All YOLOv8-World variants receive text prompts at inference, but only the pretrained and retention configurations preserve an explicit training path for Unknown Class semantics. Failure analysis further considers class-agnostic localization, miss rate, and forced misclassification, while operational feasibility is assessed using model scale, parameter count, GFLOPs, and inference latency.

Table~\ref{tab:comprehensive_map50_results} reports $mAP_{50}$ across all-class, known-class, and unknown-class evaluation. In the fully supervised 10-class setting, YOLOv8-World-L achieves 33.50\% all-class $mAP_{50}$, exceeding YOLOv8-L at 31.77\%. This confirms that prompt-conditioned visual-semantic alignment remains competitive even when both architectures receive full category supervision. The known-class rows further show strong vehicle adaptation for all fine-tuned models: the YOLOv8-World retention model reaches 52.18\% known-class $mAP_{50}$ at the Large scale, while YOLOv8-World 10-Class reaches 53.24\%. Thus, retaining unknown-category semantics does not prevent the detector from learning the dominant vehicle classes.

The open-vocabulary behavior is most visible in the unknown-class block. YOLOv8-World Zero-Shot provides a non-zero reference of 14.79\% unknown-class $mAP_{50}$ at the Large scale, whereas both YOLOv8 3-Class and ordinary YOLOv8-World 3-Class collapse to 0.00\% on Unknown Classes. This indicates that target-only fine-tuning can erase or suppress open-vocabulary responses when unknown regions are treated purely as background. The retention variant mitigates this effect: YOLOv8-World 3-Class Retention recovers 14.04\% unknown-class $mAP_{50}$ at the Large scale, only 0.75 percentage points below the zero-shot reference, while improving all-class performance from 21.45\% to 26.86\%. This trade-off is central to the proposed retention strategy because it preserves most unknown-hazard awareness while still adapting the model to BDD10K vehicle targets.

Fig.~\ref{fig:fig_yolo_result_comparison} qualitatively supports the quantitative pattern. The retention model maintains detections for semantically prompted road objects while also localizing target vehicles, whereas the 3-class closed-set configuration remains vulnerable to silent hazard omission. For autonomous driving, this distinction is important because an unknown pedestrian, traffic light, or sign that is not detected cannot be considered by downstream path planning or emergency braking modules.

\subsection{Discussion}



The comparison in Table~\ref{tab:related_work_comparison} positions the proposed experiments relative to prior object-detection studies. BDD10K ADAS and downsampling studies provide closed-set references \cite{10605392,11376542}, while the BDD100K model zoo reports 10-class Box AP values of 32.71\%, 33.72\%, and 31.13\% for Faster R-CNN R-101-FPN, Cascade R-CNN R-50-FPN, and FCOS R-101-FPN \cite{bdd100kModelZoo}. Although these references are not identical to the curated BDD10K protocol used here, they contextualize the proposed results. Ordinary YOLOv8-World-L 3-Class fine-tuning produces only 17.24\% all-class $mAP_{50}$ because unknown regions are removed from supervision, whereas retention training raises this to 26.86\%. YOLOv8-World-L 10-Class remains the supervised upper bound at 33.50\% $mAP_{50}$, while retention provides a middle ground between zero-shot prompting and full manual annotation.

These results translate directly into autonomous-driving safety implications. A planner consumes object hypotheses rather than benchmark scores, so reducing Class-Agnostic Miss Rates is a prerequisite for robust decision-making. A closed-set detector with 0.00\% unknown-class $mAP_{50}$ can silently remove pedestrians, traffic lights, or riders from the world model, leaving emergency-braking and planning modules with incomplete scene state. Open-vocabulary prompting mitigates this failure by allowing the perception system to query safety-critical concepts beyond the supervised label set.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presents Open-Vocabulary Hazard Perception for Autonomous Driving, a comparative study of YOLOv8, YOLOv8-World, and YOLOv8-World retention training on BDD10K. YOLOv8-World-L reaches 33.50\% $mAP_{50}$ in the fully supervised 10-class setting, compared with 31.77\% for YOLOv8-L. More importantly, the Target-Class experiments show that removing unknown-object supervision can collapse unknown-class detection to 0.00\%, even for an open-vocabulary model, when unknown regions are absorbed as background during fine-tuning.

The proposed retention scheme addresses the safety problem introduced in Section~I by combining manually retained three-class vehicle labels with pseudo-labels from pretrained YOLOv8-World for the seven Unknown Classes. At the Large scale, YOLOv8-World 3-Class Retention recovers 14.04\% unknown-class $mAP_{50}$, close to the 14.79\% zero-shot reference, and improves all-class $mAP_{50}$ from 21.45\% to 26.86\%. The main contributions are a controlled BDD10K Target/Unknown protocol, a scale-wise YOLOv8 and YOLOv8-World comparison, a retention strategy for preserving unknown-class awareness, and a joint quantitative and qualitative analysis. Future work should extend retention to larger BDD100K splits, adverse conditions, temporal detection, uncertainty calibration, prompt optimization, and planning simulation.
